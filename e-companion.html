Electronic Companion

This electronic companion (EC) develops in full the material that Sections 6.7 and 6.8 of the paper report in summary form. Section EC.1 presents the cross-generator replication of the complementarity census and traces how the anchored complement positions behave under the primary, replication, and consensus censuses (Table EC.1). Section EC.2 derives the reliability statistics for the auxiliary tags and explains why the product/service type tag supports analysis while the four complementarity-dimension tags do not (Table EC.2). Section EC.3 develops the structure of the complement graph itself: the hub categories that concentrate incoming complement links (Figure EC.1), the fifteen activity ecosystems into which each graph resolves (Figure EC.2), the position of those ecosystems on the embedding map (Figure EC.3), and the rare mutual pairs in which each category names the other (Figure EC.4).

Every statistic quoted here is read from the canonical results files that also generate the tables in the paper, and the graph exhibits are built deterministically from the published census files (community detection seeded at 42), so each number and each figure can be reproduced from the companion materials. All electronic companion materials — data, code, interactive graphs, and full-resolution versions of every exhibit — are maintained in full on the anonymized companion website (https://semofcomp.github.io/soc-companion/).

Cross-generator replication · EC.1

Cross-Generator Replication and Consensus Positions

The complement census is generated, so any single-generator result could in principle reflect that generator’s idiosyncrasies rather than properties of complementarity. The design therefore runs the entire census pipeline twice: once under the primary generator (GPT-4o) and once, independently and with no adjudication between the two, under a generator from a different model family (Gemini-2.5-Pro). The replication reuses the identical rubric, prompts, lexical resolution step, and mechanical validation rules, and nothing in either pass consults the embedding spaces in which the pairs are later scored. The primary census yields 15,925 focal–complement pairs across 2,896 Canadian focal categories and 18,312 pairs across 3,321 U.S. focal categories; the replication census yields 13,192 pairs (2,730 focals) and 13,241 pairs (2,689 focals), respectively.

The consensus subset is the intersection of the two censuses: a directed focal–complement pair enters only if both generators independently selected it. This is a deliberately conservative construction — with each generator keeping at most six complements per focal, sampling variability alone guarantees imperfect overlap — and it retains 3,126 pairs across 1,591 Canadian focals and 2,338 pairs across 1,182 U.S. focals. Positions are measured on each representation’s own anchored scale, defined in Section 4.3 of the paper: 0 is the representation’s mean cosine similarity over 10,000 random category pairs, 100 is its mean over taxonomic substitutes, and a pair set’s position is the share of that random-to-substitute distance covered by its mean cosine. Brackets in Table EC.1 are 95% bootstrap confidence intervals clustered on the focal category (B = 2,000).

Three regularities organize the table. First, the ordering of the four representations is invariant: in all six census–economy combinations, TF-IDF places complements lowest, the two contextual transformers sit close together in the middle, and GloVe, the co-occurrence model, sits highest. The cross-representation contrast the paper builds on — the co-occurrence model reading complements as markedly more similar than the transformers do — is therefore not an artifact of the generator that produced the pairs. Second, the two transformers remain statistically indistinguishable from each other in every census (for example, 37.2 against 37.4 in the Canadian replication census), so the contrast is carried by the co-occurrence mechanism itself rather than by any transformer-specific quirk.

Third, levels rise as the census sharpens, in a pattern that is informative rather than troubling. Within each economy, every representation places the replication census somewhat higher than the primary census, and the consensus subset markedly higher than either (under Gemini: 30.6, 37.2, and 46.0 in Canada; 41.3, 49.0, and 76.5 in the United States). Pairs that two independent generators both select behave like a higher-precision complement set — the most prototypical joint-use relations — and every representation registers that added precision as added similarity. The shift is larger in the United States because the U.S. intersection is more selective, retaining 12.8% of primary pairs against 19.6% in Canada. What matters for the paper’s claims is what does not move: the ordering of the representations, the near-identity of the two transformers, and the Canadian gap between GloVe and the transformers (15.9 points in the primary census, 20.7 in the consensus subset; the United States shows the same ordering on a compressed scale) all survive the change of generator. The positions the paper interprets are properties of the representations, not of GPT-4o.

Relative to Section 6.7 of the paper, which reports the primary and consensus positions, Table EC.1 adds the replication-census column and the interval estimates, so the stability described above can be verified representation by representation.

Auxiliary tags · EC.2

Reliability of the Auxiliary Tags

In the census’s selection pass, the generator tags each chosen pair with a product/service type and with one or more of four complementarity dimensions (functional, sequential, occasion, and demand spillover). The tags are auxiliary — no headline result conditions on them — but their reliability is consequential: the type tag partitions the census for the product/service analyses of Section 6.6 of the paper, so it must be trustworthy, whereas the dimension tags serve only as descriptive annotation. Reliability is assessed where it can be observed: on the shared (consensus) pairs that both generators selected — 3,126 in Canada, 2,338 in the United States, 5,464 pooled — by comparing the two generators’ independent tags for the same pair.

Raw agreement overstates reliability whenever a label is common, because two raters can agree by chance alone. Cohen’s κ corrects for this. With observed agreement po and chance agreement pe — the agreement expected if the two raters applied their own base rates independently — κ = (po − pe)/(1 − pe): the share of the room above chance that the raters actually earn. For the type tag, pooled across economies, the generators agree on 96.2% of shared pairs; because services are the majority class for both, independent tagging at the observed base rates would already agree 52.1% of the time, so κ = (96.2 − 52.1)/(100 − 52.1) = 0.92 — almost perfect by conventional benchmarks. Per economy, agreement is 95.6% in Canada (κ = 0.91) and 96.9% in the United States (κ = 0.93). The type tag is, in short, as reliable as the two-generator design can certify, and the product/service partition it induces can bear analytic weight.

The four dimension tags do not clear the same bar: κ ranges from 0.05 to 0.27 despite raw agreement of 46.7–81.6%. The prevalence columns of Table EC.2 locate the cause. The two generators apply the dimension labels at very different base rates: on the pooled shared pairs, GPT-4o tags demand spillover on 17.9% of pairs while Gemini tags it on 54.7%, and sequential splits 25.2% against 38.3%. Divergent base rates cap κ mechanically — observed agreement cannot exceed the overlap of the two marginal distributions — so with the demand-spillover marginals above, κ could not exceed 0.31 even if the generators agreed on every pair their base rates jointly allow. Functional shows the complementary failure mode: both generators apply it to most pairs (87.7% and 80.6%), so chance agreement is already 73.1%, and the observed 79.3% earns a κ of only 0.23 despite being the highest raw agreement among the dimensions.

These are threshold disagreements, not noise. The generators largely concur on which pairs are complements — that is what the consensus subset conditions on — and on what the paired offerings are, as the near-perfect type tag shows; they disagree on which facet of joint use to foreground when several apply, and the dimensions are non-exclusive by design, so nothing forces the two generators toward a common tagging rate. A pair one generator reads as functional joint use, the other may record as a demand spillover arising from the same relation.

The inferential division of labor follows directly. The type tag is load-bearing: it partitions the census reliably and underpins the product/service contrasts of Section 6.6 of the paper. The dimension tags are descriptive only, and no result in the paper or in this companion conditions on them. Beyond the pooled summary in Section 6.7 of the paper, Table EC.2 reports the per-economy agreement rates and κ values together with the per-generator prevalences that explain them.

The complement graph · EC.3

The Structure of the Complement Graph

The census induces a directed graph. Nodes are the detailed categories that enter at least one census pair — 2,988 of 3,049 in Canada and 3,421 of 3,520 in the United States — and each census link contributes a directed edge from focal to complement: 15,925 edges in Canada and 18,312 in the United States (densities 0.0018 and 0.0016). The build is deterministic. Hub statistics read the directed graph directly; community detection runs weighted Louvain (python-louvain, seed 42, nodes visited in sorted order) on the undirected projection, with an edge weighted 2 when the link is mutual and 1 otherwise, so the partition reported here is exactly reproducible. Section 6.8 of the paper uses this graph for a single purpose — to show that its structure does not inflate the paper’s results — and defers the structure itself to the companion. The four sections below develop it: where incoming links concentrate (Figure EC.1), how the graph decomposes into communities (Figure EC.2), where those communities sit on the embedding map (Figure EC.3), and what the rare mutual links mark (Figure EC.4).

EC.3.1. Hub Complements

A category’s in-degree is the number of focal categories whose census list names it as a complement. Incoming links are highly concentrated. The Canadian census spreads its 15,925 links over only 1,715 distinct complement categories — 9.3 incoming links each, on average — but the top category, Drafting services, is named by 414 focals (2.6% of all pairs), followed by Binding services (238), Construction services (175), Parking services (172), and Project management services (154); the five most-named categories absorb 7.2% of all census links. The United States repeats the pattern (18,312 links over 1,709 distinct complements, 10.7 each on average): Wholesale sales of packing and packaging materials and supplies leads with 459 incoming links, ahead of Industrial design services (257), Freight transportation arrangement and customs brokering services (212), Colocation services (168), and Environmental assessment services (151), the top five absorbing 6.8% of links. Figure EC.1 shows the 25 most-named categories in each economy, colored by NAPCS section.

The hub list reads as a roster of widely required inputs and enabling services. What many different activities need is not one another’s outputs but the same cross-cutting infrastructure: design and documentation (drafting, industrial design), packaging and freight, construction and project management, parking and colocation. These are the categories whose demand is derived from the broadest range of activities — the census’s picture of general-purpose complements. Nor are the hubs exclusively services: goods categories such as Industrial sand (125 incoming links in Canada) reach the top ranks, and both economies draw hubs from goods sections as well as service sections. For the paper’s measurement question the hubs matter chiefly as a threat — had these recurrent categories also sat unusually close to everything in embedding space, they could have manufactured the aggregate signal — and Section 6.8 shows the opposite: pairs involving top-decile hubs score lower than other pairs in every representation (mean percentile rank 0.779 against 0.866 under Gemini in Canada). What Figure EC.1 adds beyond Section 6.8 is the identity and composition of the hubs themselves, which the paper reports only as summary statistics.

EC.3.2. Activity Ecosystems

Louvain community detection partitions the graph so that links fall within communities far more often than between them, and modularity Q measures its success: the share of the graph’s linkage falling within communities beyond what a degree-matched random graph would place there (Q = 0 means no more structure than chance; values approaching 1 mean nearly separable subgraphs). Both complement graphs resolve into 15 communities at almost identical modularity — Q = 0.555 in Canada and Q = 0.542 in the United States — so the census is organized into well-separated neighborhoods of jointly used categories rather than a single undifferentiated web, and to nearly the same degree in two independently generated economies.

The communities are activity ecosystems, not taxonomy branches. Each mixes NAPCS sections: the mean entropy of members’ top-level sections is 2.32 bits in Canada against 2.92 bits for random member sets of the same sizes (1.34 against 1.74 in the United States) — far below random, so the communities are real, yet far above zero, so no community is a supply-side section in disguise. Named examples make the composition concrete. Canada’s largest community (476 members) is an industrial-materials ecosystem gathering Industrial sand, Industrial spraying equipment, Welding and soldering equipment, machine-tool attachments, and Metal heat treating services: goods from four goods sections joined by the wholesale lines and industrial services that work alongside them. A 90-member community is the graph’s financial-services ecosystem (Financial planning and investment management, Financial auditing services, Financial management consulting services, Insurance consulting services) and, at 0.79 bits, the most section-pure community in the Canadian graph. An 87-member community assembles crop production: mixed fertilizers, Fertilizer materials of organic origin, Water delivered by water works and irrigation systems, and Soil management, crop and animal production consulting services — farm inputs, a utility, and an advisory service that no single section of the classification contains. The United States shows the same anatomy: a 616-member logistics-and-business-operations ecosystem (Freight transportation arrangement and customs brokering services, Colocation services, Towing services, Data management services), a 510-member construction ecosystem (Environmental assessment services, architectural services, Construction project management services, drafting for civil engineering), and a 252-member print-production ecosystem joining letterpress and flexographic printing inks to Printing plates and cylinders, Advertising creative services, and Web site design and development services.

Figure EC.2 profiles all fifteen communities in each economy, ordered by size and annotated with the entropy of members’ sections. Beyond Section 6.8 of the paper, which reports only the community count and modularity, the composition shown here identifies what the communities are: demand-side bundles that cut across the supply-side classification.

EC.3.3. Ecosystems on the Category Map

Figure EC.3 places the fifteen communities on the two-dimensional UMAP projection of the Gemini embedding space used in Figure 1 of the paper, with categories outside the complement graph in light grey (61 in Canada, 99 in the United States). The projection arranges categories by semantic similarity, so if the demand-side communities coincided with the geometry’s own cluster structure, each color would occupy a compact patch. They do not: every community spreads across broad, overlapping swathes of the map and routinely spans several of the visible section clusters.

The figure is the graph-level image of the paper’s central asymmetry. The embedding map organizes categories by what they are; the communities collect categories by what they are used with; and the two organizations disagree — the fertilizer, the irrigation water, and the agronomy consultancy of the Canadian crop ecosystem sit in different regions of the map yet belong to one demand-side bundle. Beyond Section 6.8 of the paper, which never visualizes the communities, Figure EC.3 shows directly that ecosystem structure is not recoverable from the cluster structure of the supply-side geometry — the conclusion the paper reaches through its cosine analyses, read here from the map.

EC.3.4. Reciprocity and Mutual Pairs

A directed edge from focal to complement is reciprocated when the reverse edge is also in the census — when each category names the other. Reciprocity is rare: 530 of Canada’s 15,925 directed edges (3.33%) are reciprocated, folding into 265 unordered mutual pairs, and 324 of 18,312 in the United States (1.77%), or 162 pairs. Part of the scarcity is mechanical, since each focal keeps at most six complements and a reverse link can fail merely by ranking seventh; its magnitude, however, is economic. Complementarity, as the census expresses it, is overwhelmingly directional: many activities need drafting services, packaging, or freight, while those categories’ own best complements lie elsewhere, so the dependence runs one way.

When reciprocity does occur, it marks the census’s tightest bilateral relations. Canada’s mutual pairs include Accounting services and Financial management consulting services, Advertising graphic design services and Commercial and industrial photography services, and mixed fertilizers and Water delivered by water works and irrigation systems; U.S. examples include Adoption services and Legal services, family law, and Advertising creative services and Postproduction services for audiovisual works. In each, one side’s activity presupposes the other’s — standing partnerships rather than one-way requirements. The geometry registers the difference: under Gemini, the mean complement percentile rank (the share of all other categories that a complement outranks in cosine similarity to its focal) is 97.7 for mutual pairs against 81.6 for one-way pairs in Canada, and 97.2 against 74.8 in the United States (Figure EC.4, panel (b) of each row). Mutual selection thus behaves as the census’s internal marker of its most robust relations, and the embedding spaces agree with it.

Section 6.8 of the paper reports the Canadian reciprocity rate and the mutual-versus-one-way contrast in a single sentence; Figure EC.4 gives the counts and the full contrast for both economies, and the companion website’s reciprocity explorer lists every mutual pair with its tags and cosine similarities.
