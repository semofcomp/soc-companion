What text embeddings know about complementarity

Goods and services used together are present in embedding space — but not along the axis cosine similarity reads. Each section below carries its own exhibits and its full analysis; move through them in order, or jump to any one.

The phenomenon · 1 of 7

Used together, not alike

Complementarity is the relationship between things consumed or produced together — bread and butter, lumber and nails, a flight and a hotel. It is not similarity, and the difference is the whole point. A category's substitutes are the things like it, the rivals you would choose instead; its complements are the things unlike it that you reach for alongside.

That split is visible in embedding space to the naked eye. Place every category by its embedding and a focal category's substitutes cluster tightly around it, while its complements scatter across the map — near in use, far in description.

What counts as each

Substitutes are same‑class siblings — NAPCS neighbors sharing a six‑digit parent, interchangeable in use. Complements are cross‑class pairs an LLM census (GPT‑4o, ≈5.5 per focal) names as used together. The two are constructed to be disjoint, which is exactly what makes the test in §3 clean.

Across all 3,049 categories this scattering is the rule, not the exception — and the whole structure is browsable in the 3‑D map.

The pathology · 2 of 7

Cosine mistakes complements for strangers

If complements scatter, then cosine similarity — the default way to compare embeddings — should struggle to find them. It does not merely struggle; it fails systematically, and in an informative way.

Rank every relationship type by its mean cosine and a clean gradient appears: random pairs lowest, complements barely above them, then same‑group, then same‑class, with substitutes highest of all. Complements sit down near the noise floor — the things a category is used with look, to cosine, almost as unrelated as a category drawn at random.

The cosine gradient

Mean cosine by relationship type, per representation.

How to read it

Five bars per embedding; bar height is the average cosine similarity for that relationship. Read left to right: random pairs (grey) set the floor, complements (teal) sit barely above it, and substitutes (coral) tower over both. The gap that matters is teal‑vs‑coral — by cosine, a category's complements look almost as unrelated to it as random noise, while its substitutes look highly related. Cosine is measuring the wrong thing.

Turned into a decision the failure is not weak but inverted. Asked to separate complements from substitutes on cosine alone, every representation scores an AUC below the 0.50 coin‑flip line — worst of all for the transformer embeddings that are otherwise the strongest:

Why the transformers fail hardest

Gemini and OpenAI post the lowest cosine AUC (≈0.06). Their geometry packs same‑class substitutes extremely tightly — a strong similarity prior that is precisely what you want for synonymy and precisely what buries the cross‑class complement signal deepest. The sharper a model's sense of "alike," the more thoroughly complementarity is hidden — which is why, in §3, the probe recovers the most exactly there.

An AUC of 0.06 is not ignorance; it is confident error. By cosine, the average complement looks less related to its focal than a category drawn at random.

The fix · 3 of 7

Off the cosine axis

That leaves a sharp question. Is complementarity simply absent from the embedding — a real ceiling on what distributional meaning can carry — or is it present, just not along the one axis cosine reads? We test the second directly, and deliberately weakly.

We train a thin linear read‑out: a logistic regression over the same Gemini vectors, given only each pair's elementwise product and absolute difference, scored strictly out‑of‑fold so no focal appears in both training and test. One linear boundary — no new embedding, no fine‑tuning, no extra data.

The off‑axis scatter

Every pair by cosine (x) and probe score (y). Hover any dot to read the pair.

How to read it

Every dot is one pair — teal a focal and one of its complements, coral two same‑class substitutes. Left–right is raw cosine (how alike the embedding thinks they are); up–down is the probe's learned verdict (how complement‑like). Scan across and the colors are interleaved — cosine cannot separate them. Scan up and they fall into two bands — the probe can. Same dots, two yardsticks. The dashed line is the probe's decision threshold: call everything above it a complement and you are right 83% of the time.

Read left–right (cosine) the two classes sit on top of one another — that is the 0.08. Read up–down (the probe) they fall into two clean bands, split by the dashed cut‑off that alone recovers 83% balanced accuracy. The information was in the vectors the whole time; cosine just collapses the single direction that carries it.

Complementarity lives in the geometry — only not along the direction similarity reads.

The same gap as ROC area

Area under each curve = AUC.

How to read it — and what AUC means

AUC, in plain terms, is the chance that if you draw one complement and one substitute at random, the measure scores the complement higher. The probe's 0.91 means it ranks the complement above the substitute 91% of the time — almost always right. Cosine's 0.08 means it does so only 8% of the time — almost always wrong: a score below 0.50 is not "no signal," it is reversed signal (flip cosine's calls and you'd score 0.92). Each point on a curve is one threshold; the curve trades catching complements (up) against false alarms on substitutes (right). Perfect hugs the top‑left corner, the diagonal is a coin flip — and cosine bows the wrong way, below it.

As area under the ROC curve the gap is stark: the probe bows up to 0.91 while raw cosine sags below the chance diagonal to 0.08. Cosine is not blind to the comparison — it is inverted by it.

How the read-out is built

Logistic regression on the elementwise product and |difference| of anisotropy‑corrected, PCA‑300 Gemini vectors — complement pairs against same‑subclass substitute pairs. Grouped out‑of‑fold by focal (GroupKFold), so the 0.91 is held‑out generalization, not memorization. The dashed line is Youden's optimal cut‑off; above it, a pair is called a complement.

Generality · 4 of 7

It survives every cut

A result this sharp invites a deflationary worry: is it an artifact of one economy, or one kind of category? We cut the data four ways — Canada and the United States, each split into consumption and production categories — and re‑ran the entire pathology‑and‑probe test in every cell.

Economy × domain cross‑survival

Cosine AUC (pathology) vs. probe AUC (recovery), Gemini.

How to read it

One tile per slice of the data (economy × domain). In each, the short coral bar is the cosine AUC and the long teal bar is the probe AUC; 0.50 is the coin‑flip line. What matters is the repetition — coral stays well below 0.50 and teal well above it in all four tiles at once. The result does not depend on which economy or which kind of category you look at.

In all four cells the same shape holds: cosine well below 0.50, the probe well above. The magnitudes move — production is cleaner than consumption, Canada cleaner than the US — but the direction never does.

Consumption vs. production exemplars

Canada is the richer consumption exemplar; the United States, roughly two‑thirds producer‑framed, is the production exemplar — and the finding survives the framing in both. Complementarity‑off‑the‑axis is not a quirk of one market or one accounting tradition. The interactive version is on the Generality page.

Robustness & transparency · 5 of 7

Pressure-tested

Beyond the 2×2, the result is stressed along three further axes — representation, generator, and dose — and the whole audit is kept in the open.

Representation. The pathology‑and‑recovery holds across all four embeddings, and is sharpest exactly where it should be: the two transformers are the worst on cosine and the best under the probe.

Representation report card

Cosine vs. probe AUC across four embeddings.

How to read it

One row per embedding. The cosine column is how well raw similarity separates complements from substitutes (all far below 0.50 — the pathology); the probe column is how well the learned read‑out does (all far above); "gain" is the distance between them. The two transformer rows are the tell — they have the lowest cosine and the highest probe. The failure and the fix are sharpest in exactly the same place.

Dose. The signal is graded, not binary: the higher the generator ranked a complement, the higher its cosine percentile — a smooth decline from first‑listed to sixth across every representation.

Dose–response

Mean cosine percentile by the generator's rank of the complement.

How to read it

Each line is one embedding. The x‑axis is how early the generator listed a complement (1 = named first, most confident); the y‑axis is that complement's average cosine percentile. The lines slope down left‑to‑right — complements the generator was surest of also sit higher by similarity. A graded dose–response like this is hard to fake: it means the census is tracking a real, continuous signal rather than flipping a coin.

Generator agreement (κ)

Re‑eliciting the whole census with Gemini agrees with GPT‑4o on the product/service label at κ = 0.91 (95.6% raw agreement) — "almost perfect" by the usual benchmarks — and the GPT‑4o ∩ Gemini consensus census reproduces every headline. The finding is not an artifact of one model's idiosyncrasies.

External validation · 6 of 7

Does the world agree?

The census is model‑elicited, so the last question is whether it tracks anything outside the model. We check it against two kinds of external ground truth: behavior and the economy.

Behavior. Amazon co‑purchase (also‑buy / also‑view lift) and Yelp co‑occurrence both recover the complement signal above chance. Economy. The StatCan and BEA input–output accounts — independent, authoritative maps of what industries actually use together — agree as well.

!
The limits are stated plainly, not buried: US production validation leans on the Canadian StatCan tables, with BEA reproduced under crosswalk limits; and US Amazon concordance is attenuated by coarse category mapping. The convergent evidence is real and the caveats are documented — full detail on the Validation page.

The structure it unlocks · 7 of 7

A map of the economy's complements

Once complementarity is recoverable, the census stops being a list and becomes a graph — every category pointing to the things used alongside it. That graph turns out to have real economic structure.

It partitions into fifteen dense community‑clusters at high modularity (0.55) — coherent supply‑and‑use neighborhoods, not noise. A handful of hub categories anchor whole chains: Drafting services alone is named a complement by 414 others. And complementarity is overwhelmingly one‑directional — only 3.3% of links are mutual, the rare pairs that each name the other (accounting and financial‑management consulting among them).
